ChatGPT's take on Front-End coding?

21 Nov 2023

With the turn of the decade AI has sprung into the spotlight and it only makes sense that it has appeared in the world of academia as well. AI Chat bots such as ChatGPT can find relevance in any field of learning but for software engineering it proves useful for coders in a multitude of ways. I myself have begun making use of AI for the past year for various uses between courses or personal projects. ChatGPT has been the resource I use for most of my inquiries when I need specific information on a topic that sometimes is hard to search for through typical web searches.

Looking back on my personal use for AI within ICS 314, there have been many cases where I had asked for its assistance when coding:

  1. Experience WODs e.g. E18
    No usage of AI, if I ever needed assistance I would turn to the provided tutorials or ask my classmates.

  2. In-class Practice WODs
    Would occasionally use it for coding assistance, but I felt like the practice WODs are more useful if I attempt them without AI help.

  3. In-class WODs
    I did in fact use ChatGPT to assist me with these WODs. However, since most of front end coding is based on artistic vision, there were many cases where the given code from ChatGPT would not be applicable with what was described for the desired end product in the WOD. Thus, I would have to deviate from the suggested code from the AI. An example of usage during a WOD would be giving the AI context for the website and what I’m trying to implement then asking “How would I implement a navbar that has […] options on the left side and […] options on the right.”

  4. Essays
    The essays did not have any ChatGPT influence as many of the topics that we would have to write about depended on our own experiences within the course.

  5. Final project
    I have yet to use AI coding for the final project but if something comes up that I find myself stuck at I might turn to ChatGPT for troubleshooting purposes.

  6. Learning a concept / tutorial
    Not within this course but I have definitely used AI as a starting point to learn a new concept, and used it to guide me for further google searches. I will get into this at a later part in this essay.

  7. Answering a question in class or in Discord
    No have not used AI for either of these, I feel like those questions should be answered from personal experience.

  8. Asking or answering a smart-question
    Likewise with #7, these should be answered by someone who personally fixed the issue. Otherwise they could just utilize ChatGPT themselves.

  9. Coding example e.g. “give an example of using Underscore .pluck”
    I rarely used ChatGPT for this as the material we were provided in the course was often sufficient in teaching how to use new programs and packages.

  10. Explaining code
    Yes I have used ChatGPT many times within this course and outside of this course to explain code, often when things were not working as intended. There have even been instances where the code works perfectly fine but I still ask ChatGPT to explain why as to gain a better understanding of how the program works. An example of this would be “Can you explain why this component ends up underneath my image even though I tell it to show on the right? copy paste code”

  11. Writing code
    I have also used ChatGPT for writing code. As for 314, which focuses on the front end, I have found ChatGPT useful for laying down the foundations of the pages I am creating. Afterwards I go in and make edits to the features of the page such as width of a component or spacing of some buttons. An example of this would be “I am trying to create a submit button in the footer of my page. I would like there to be a text box with some default text ‘Enter email…’ that clears once a user starts writing in the box. Can you tell me how to add this to my page?”

  12. Documenting code
    I have not really used ChatGPT for documentation. I feel like it makes more sense to do that as an individual.

  13. Quality assurance e.g. “What’s wrong with this code ” or “Fix the ESLint errors in ” Errors or ESLint tends to give enough explanation as to why those errors are occurring. However AI tends to be useful for other coding instances where the errors are a bit more ambiguous.

  14. Other uses in ICS 314 not listed
    Not really any outside usages besides the ones listed for ICS 314.

When it comes to my impact on learning and understanding, there definitely are pluses and minus when it comes to AI. When I want to have a better understanding of “why” something works or “how” something functions, ChatGPT has proven to be exceedingly useful for this compared to random forums online such as stack overflow as those have varying levels of usefulness when applying to one’s own code. However, when it comes to actually creating something myself from scratch, ChatGPT has made me lazy to the point where I struggle to recall if the base format for my code is fine. So in terms of coding “skill” I have weakened when using AI, but my understanding has increased.

For practical applications, I found myself using ChatGPT for many reasons outside of ICS 314. One point I wanted to touch on was that ChatGPT is very proficient for more backend coding or underlying issues of when a system breaks down. Copy and pasting the whole program into ChatGPT can result in an explanation of where things are going wrong, written in a human-like manner due to the Natural Language Processing that AI tends to utilize. However, I will provide a caveat that there is ONE course that I have that ChatGPT consistently struggles with (wrong answers 80% of the time). That course being ICS 312, Machine-Level and Systems Programming. Whenever I ask it for assistance with coding in Assembly, all understanding breaks down and it produces incorrect answers, or there’s just straight up errors when coding. I just figured this was interesting to note, maybe something about low-level code that makes it hard for AI to understand?

Challenges and limitations of AI usage within this course have been stated in the list above but I can reiterate it here: When an issue requires “artistic vision” such as preference with how some component should look, or sizing and spacing of buttons ChatGPT “struggles”. I say struggle because it doesn’t necessarily give wrong answers, but it cannot understand exactly what the user has in mind, since it’s all about seeing or imagining something and putting it into action. Like if you were to imagine a scene of a lake below a hill, you can put into words what comes to your mind. However, no matter how well you try to explain it to an AI (or even someone else for that matter) they will usually think of something similar but with some slight differences. It’s just hard to get across the exact same imagery that comes to mind using only words. Comparative analysis between traditional teaching vs AI oriented teaching when it comes to software engineering can be analyzed through different pain points of learning. For engagement and knowledge retention I feel as though the levels are the same, as there are still considerations put in as a student when trying to learn these new techniques or programs. But when it comes to skill development I feel like students who over use AI can find themselves lacking, due to much of the problem solving being done by the AI rather than the student.

Looking towards future considerations, I feel as though this class took the right approach and allowed students to use AI as much as they felt comfortable with to augment their learning experience. I feel as though in the past, some fields of academia had refused to adapt to the changing environment, such is the case in the early 2000’s when calculators started to become more and more accessible. It was deemed wrong by some math teachers to use calculators when solving problems as student’s would not always have access to one in the future. But fast forward a few years and suddenly almost everyone has access to one as they are all integrated into everyday phones. It always seemed wrong to me to refuse access to new technologies when it seems more useful to just accept it and learn how to use it proficiently. Such is the case for this course. I have already found many uses for AI when it comes to coding, but there are certain edge cases where it proves faulty, and overall it can serve as a better learning experience to attempt to code assignments without the help of AI for better problem solving skills.

Overall, AI is becoming ever more commonplace in today’s society and it would be wrong to try and refuse its usage in everyday life. This includes jobs such as software engineering and coding purposes. I would maintain this course’s approach with allowing the usage of AI for coding but propose that for practice WODs or other smaller coding experiences that the students attempt them without the usage of AI, just to ensure they develop a decent amount of front-end coding skills.
